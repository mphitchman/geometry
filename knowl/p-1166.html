To determine the distance \(d_S(p,q)\) between arbitrary points in \(\mathbb{P}^2\text{,}\) we may first apply a transformation in \(\cal{S}\) that sends \(p\) to the origin, and \(q\) to a point on the positive real axis. By an appropriate choice of \(\theta\text{,}\) the transformation

\begin{equation*} T(z) = e^{i\theta}\frac{z-p}{1+\overline{p}z} \end{equation*}

will do the trick. Now, \(T\) is a MÃ¶bius transformation of the entire extended plane, and it may take \(q\) outside the unit disk, in which case \(q_a\) will be mapped to a point on the real axis inside the unit disk. So, either \(|T(q)|\) or \(|T(q_a)|\) will be a real number between 0 and 1. If we call this number \(x\text{,}\) then \(d_S(p,q)=d_S(0,x)\text{,}\) since transformations in \(\cal{S}\) preserve distance between points.
