Solution 3.5.10.1.

Suppose \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) are non-intersecting clines in \(\mathbb{C}\text{.}\) Then at least one of them is a circle since two lines intersect at \(\infty\text{.}\) By TheoremÂ 3.2.16 there are two points, say \(p\) and \(q\text{,}\) that are symmetric with respect to both clines. At least one of these points must be finite, say \(p\text{.}\) Consider the \mob \(T(z) = \frac{1}{z-p}\text{.}\) This map takes \(p\) to \(\infty\text{,}\) and sends \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) to two circles. Since \(T\) also preserves symmetry points, and \(p\) gets sent to \(\infty\text{,}\) it follows that \(q\) must get sent to the center of each new circle, forcing the two circles to be concentric.
