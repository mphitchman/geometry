To arrive at an arc-length differential, we want to let \(\Delta t\) approach 0. As this happens, \(T(\boldsymbol{r}(t+\Delta t))\) approaches \(T(\boldsymbol{r}(t))\text{,}\) which is 0. From calculus we also know that \(\ln(1+x) \approx x\) for \(x\) very close to 0. Thus, for small \(\Delta t\text{,}\) we have

\begin{align*} d_H(\boldsymbol{r}(t),\boldsymbol{r}(t+\Delta t)) \amp \approx |T(\boldsymbol{r}(t+\Delta t))| + |T(\boldsymbol{r}(t+\Delta t))|\\ \amp =2\cdot \bigg| \frac{\boldsymbol{r}(t+\Delta t) - \boldsymbol{r}(t)}{1 - \overline{\boldsymbol{r}(t)}\boldsymbol{r}(t+\Delta t)}\bigg |\\ \amp =2\cdot \frac{\big | \frac{\boldsymbol{r}(t+\Delta t) - \boldsymbol{r}(t)}{\Delta t}\big |}{|1 - \overline{\boldsymbol{r}(t)}\boldsymbol{r}(t+\Delta t)|}\cdot | \Delta t |. \end{align*}
