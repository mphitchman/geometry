We first show that any given hyperbolic line \(L\) is congruent to the hyperbolic line on the real axis. Suppose \(p\) is a point on \(L\text{,}\) and \(v\) is one of its ideal points. By LemmaÂ 5.1.5 there is a transformation \(T\) in \({\cal H}\) that maps \(p\) to 0, \(v\) to 1, and \(p^*\) to \(\infty\text{.}\) Thus \(T(L)\) is the portion of the real axis inside \(\mathbb{D}\text{,}\) and \(L\) is congruent to the hyperbolic line on the real axis. Since any hyperbolic line is congruent to the hyperbolic line on the real axis, the group nature of \(\cal H\) ensures that any two hyperbolic lines are congruent.
