In the hyperbolic plane, we may deduce the arc-length differential by a similar argument. Suppose \(\boldsymbol{r}\) is a smooth curve in \(\mathbb{D}\) given by \(\boldsymbol{r}(t) = x(t) + iy(t)\text{,}\) for \(a \leq t \leq b\text{.}\) One may approximate the length of a tiny portion of the curve, say from \(\boldsymbol{r}(t)\) to \(\boldsymbol{r}(t + \Delta t)\text{,}\) by the hyperbolic distance between these two points, \(d_H(\boldsymbol{r}(t), \boldsymbol{r}(t + \Delta t))\text{.}\) To compute this distance, we first send the point \(\boldsymbol{r}(t)\) to 0 by the transformation

\begin{equation*} T(z) = \frac{z-\boldsymbol{r}(t)}{1-\overline{\boldsymbol{r}(t)}z}\text{,} \end{equation*}

so that

\begin{equation*} d_H(\boldsymbol{r}(t),\boldsymbol{r}(t+\Delta t)) = \ln[1 + |T(\boldsymbol{r}(t+\Delta t))|]-\ln[1 - |T(\boldsymbol{r}(t+\Delta t))|]\text{.} \end{equation*}
