Proof

If \(k = 1\text{,}\) the set \(D\) is a Euclidean line, according to TheoremÂ 2.4.2, so we assume \(k \neq 1\text{.}\) Let \(C\) be the circle centered at \(p\) with radius 1. Suppose \(z\) is an arbitrary point in the set \(D\text{.}\) Inverting about \(C\text{,}\) let \(z^* = i_C(z)\) and \(q^* = i_C(q)\) as in the following diagram.

Observe first that \(\Delta pz^*q^*\) and \(\Delta pqz\) are similar.

Indeed, \(|p-z|\cdot|p-z^*|=1=|p-q|\cdot|p-q^*|\) by the definition of the inversion transformation, so we have equal side-length ratios

\begin{equation*} \frac{|p-z^*|}{|p-q|}=\frac{|p-q^*|}{|p-z|}\text{,} \end{equation*}

and the included angles are equal, \(\angle q^*pz^* = \angle qpz\text{.}\)

It follows that

\begin{equation*} \frac{|z-q|}{|p-q|}=\frac{|z^*-q^*|}{|z^*-p|}\text{,} \end{equation*}

from which we derive

\begin{align*} |z^*-q^*|\amp =|z^*-p|\cdot\frac{|z-q|}{|p-q|}\\ \amp =[|z^*-p|\cdot|z-p|]\cdot\frac{|z-q|}{|z-p|}\cdot\frac{1}{|p-q|}\\ \amp = 1 \cdot \frac{1}{k} \cdot \frac{1}{|p-q|}\text{.} \end{align*}

Thus, the set \(D\) of all points \(z\) satisfying \(|z-p|=k|z-q|\) has image \(i_C(D)\) under this inversion consisting of all points \(z^*\) on a circle centered at \(q^*\) with radius \((k|p-q|)^{-1}\text{.}\) Since inversion preserves clines and \(p\) is not on \(i_C(D)\text{,}\) it follows that \(D\) itself is a circle.
