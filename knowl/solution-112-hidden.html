First map \(z_0\) to the origin by a map \(T\) in \(\cal H\text{.}\) Construct the unique Euclidean line through 0 and the center of \(T(L)\text{.}\) By applying \(T^{-1}\) to the situation we obtain the unique hyperbolic line through \(z_0\) that is perpendicular to \(L\text{.}\)

We may also construct the perpendicular for general \(z_0\) in \(\mathbb{D}\) and any given hyperbolic line \(L\text{.}\) Any hyperbolic line through \(z_0\) must go through \(z_0^*\text{.}\) To be perpendicular to \(L\text{,}\) the line must also go through \(z_0^\prime\text{,}\) the point symmetric to \(z_0\) with respect to \(L\text{.}\) (Can \(z_0^* = z_0^\prime\text{?}\)) The unique cline through the three points \(z_0, z_0^*, z_0^\prime\) is the hyperbolic line through \(z_0\) that is perpendicular to \(L\text{.}\)
