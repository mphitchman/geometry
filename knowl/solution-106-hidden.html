We may move our hyperbolic circle so that \(p\) is at the origin. Then the hyperbolic segment connecting \(z_0\) and \(p\) is a Euclidean segment. And the Euclidean line tangent to the circle at \(p\) is also a hyperbolic line, and it is perpendicular to the radius \(z_0p\text{.}\) Since moving a cline around in the hyperbolic plane preserves angles and hyperbolic lines, the result holds for general hyperbolic circles and points \(p\text{.}\)
