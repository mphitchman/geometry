Without loss of generality, we may consider the vertex of our lune to be the origin. As before, elliptic lines through the origin must also pass through \(\infty\text{,}\) so our two lines forming the lune are Euclidean lines. After a convenient rotation, we may further assume one of these lines is the real axis, so that the lune resembles the one in FigureÂ 6.3.10. To compute the area of the lune, compute the integral

\begin{equation*} A = 2 \int_0^\alpha \int_0^{1/\sqrt{k}}\frac{4r}{(1+kr^2)^2}drd\theta. \end{equation*}

Letting \(u = 1 + kr^2\) so that \(du = 2krdr\text{,}\) the bounds of integration change from \([0,1/\sqrt{k}]\) to \([1,2]\text{.}\) Then,

\begin{equation*} A = 2 \int^\alpha_0 \frac{2}{k}\int_1^2 \frac{du}{u^2}d\theta = \frac{4}{k}\int_0^\alpha \frac{1}{2} d\theta =\frac{2\alpha}{k}. \end{equation*}

Thus, the angle of a lune with interior angle \(\alpha\) is \(2\alpha/k\text{.}\)
