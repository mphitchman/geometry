Suppose a \(\frac{2}{3}\)-ideal triangle in \(\mathbb{D}\) has ideal points \(p\) and \(q\text{,}\) and interior vertex \(r\text{.}\) Apply a hyperbolic rotation of \(\mathbb{D}\) about \(r\) that sends one of the ideal points (say \(q\) as in the following diagram) to the point 1 on the real axis, and the other ideal point to an ideal point with positive imaginary component. Then, by a parallel displacement that leaves \(1\) fixed, the second ideal point may be mapped to \(i\text{.}\) Of course this map takes \(r\) to some other point in \(\mathbb{D}\text{,}\) call it \(r^\prime\text{.}\) Finally, since \(1, i,\) and \(r^\prime\) are not on the same hyperbolic line (for otherwise we wouldn't have a \(\frac{2}{3}\)-ideal triangle), the circle through 1, \(i\text{,}\) and \(r^\prime\) intersects the real axis at a point in \(\mathbb{D}\text{.}\) Thus, there is a hyperbolic translation fixing \(1\) and \(i\) that maps \(r^\prime\) onto the real axis.
