Item 6.2.1.d.
Suppose \(p, q \in \mathbb{C}^+\text{.}\) First we prove there exists a map in \(\cal S\) that sends \(p\) to the origin. If \(p = \infty\text{,}\) the map \(T(z) = \frac{1}{z}\) sends \(p\) to \(0\text{,}\) and this map is in \(\cal S\) by part (c). If \(p \neq \infty\text{,}\) the map \(T(z)=\frac{z-p}{1+\overline{p}z}\) sends \(p\) to \(0\text{,}\) and this map is in \(\cal S\) by part (b). In either case, we have a map \(T\) in \(\cal S\) that sends \(p\) to \(0\text{.}\) Then, we may rotate about the origin to fix the origin and move \(T(q)\) to the positive real axis. This rotation is also in \(\cal S\text{,}\) so the composition will be too, and we have a map in \(\cal S\) sending \(p\) to \(0\) and \(q\) to the positive real axis.
