Example 5.5.9 The area of a \(\frac{2}{3}\)-ideal triangle

Suppose \(w \in \mathbb{U}\) is on the unit circle, and consider the \(\frac{2}{3}\)-ideal triangle \(1w\infty\) as pictured.

In particular, suppose the interior angle at \(w\) is \(\alpha\text{,}\) so that \(w = e^{i(\pi-\alpha)}\) where \(0 \lt \alpha \lt \pi\text{.}\)

The area of this \(\frac{2}{3}\)-ideal triangle is thus

\begin{align*} A \amp = \int_{\cos(\pi - \alpha)}^1 \int_{\sqrt{1-x^2}}^\infty \frac{1}{y^2}~dydx\\ \amp = \int_{\cos(\pi - \alpha)}^1 \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}~dx. \end{align*}

With the trig substituion \(\cos(\theta) = x\text{,}\) so that \(\sqrt{1-x^2} = \sin(\theta)\) and \(-\sin(\theta)d\theta = dx\text{,}\) the integral becomes

\begin{align*} \amp =\int_{\pi - \alpha}^0\frac{-\sin(\theta)}{\sin(\theta)}~d\theta\\ \amp = \pi - \alpha. \end{align*}
