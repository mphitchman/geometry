Solution 7.2.3.1.
  1. Let \(\boldsymbol{r}(t) = t + 0i\) where \(0 \leq t \leq x\) describe the line segment from 0 to \(x\) where \(0 \lt x \lt s = 1/\sqrt{k}\text{.}\) Then, with a substitution of \(u = \sqrt{k}t\) below,
    \begin{align*} {\cal L}(\boldsymbol{r}) \amp= \int_0^x \frac{2|\boldsymbol{r}^\prime(t)|}{1 + k|\boldsymbol{r}(t)|^2}~dt\\ \amp=\int_0^x \frac{2\cdot 1}{1 + kt^2}~dt\\ \amp= \frac{2}{\sqrt{k}}\int_0^x \frac{\sqrt{k}}{1+(\sqrt{k}t)^2}~dt\\ \amp=\frac{2}{\sqrt{k}}\int_0^{\sqrt{k}x}\frac{1}{1+u^2}~du\\ \amp= \frac{2}{\sqrt{k}}\arctan(\sqrt{k}x)\\ \amp= 2s \arctan\left(\frac{x}{s}\right)\text{.} \end{align*}
  2. Let \(\boldsymbol{r}(t) = x\cos(t) + x\sin(t) i\) for \(0 \leq t \leq 2\pi\) denote the circle centered at 0 with elliptic radius \(r\text{.}\) To have this (elliptic) radius, \(x\) must satisfy the equation \(r = 2s \arctan(\frac{x}{s})\text{,}\) so that \(x = s\tan(\frac{r}{2s})\text{.}\) Now,
    \begin{align*} {\cal L}(\boldsymbol{r}) \amp= \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{2|\boldsymbol{r}^\prime(t)|}{1 + k|\boldsymbol{r}(t)|^2}~dt\\ \amp=\int_0^{2\pi} \frac{2\cdot x}{1 + kx^2}~dt\\ \amp= 2\pi \frac{2\cdot x}{1 + kx^2}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Then express this circumference in terms of the elliptic radius \(r\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} {\cal L}(\boldsymbol{r}) \amp= \frac{4\pi s \tan(\frac{r}{2s})}{1+ks^2 \tan^2(\frac{r}{2s})}\\ \amp=\frac{4\pi s \tan(\frac{r}{2s})}{1+\tan^2(\frac{r}{2s})}\cdot \frac{\cos^2(\frac{r}{2s})}{\cos^2(\frac{r}{2s})}\\ \amp=\frac{4\pi s \sin(\frac{r}{2s})\cos(\frac{r}{2s})}{\cos^2(\frac{r}{2s})+\sin^2(\frac{r}{2s})}\\ \amp= 2\pi s\sin\left(\frac{r}{s}\right)\text{.} \end{align*}
  3. Suppose the circle with elliptic radius \(r\) is centered at the origin. Let \(x\) denote its Euclidean radius (and note that \(r = 2s\arctan(\frac{x}{s}))\text{.}\) The area of the circle, in terms of the Euclidean radius \(x\) is thus
    \begin{align*} A \amp= \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^x\frac{4r}{(1+kr^2)^2}~dr~d\theta\\ \amp= \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{-2}{k(1+kr^2)}\bigg|_0^x~d\theta\\ \amp= 2\pi\left(\frac{2}{k}-\frac{2}{k(1+kx^2)}\right)\\ \amp= 4\pi\frac{k^2x^2}{k^2(1+kx^2)}\\ \amp= 4\pi \frac{x^2}{1+kx^2}\text{.} \end{align*}
    To express the area in terms of the elliptic radius \(r\text{,}\) set \(x = s\tan(\frac{r}{2s})\) so that
    \begin{equation*} A = \frac{4\pi s^2\tan^2(\frac{r}{2s})}{1 + ks^2\tan^2(\frac{r}{2})}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Multiplying top and bottom by \(\cos^2(r/2s)\) and noting that \(ks^2 = 1\) it follows that
    \begin{equation*} A = 4\pi s^2 \sin^2\left(\frac{r}{2s}\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}
