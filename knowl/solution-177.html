Solution 7.4.1.1.

If \(k=0\) the transformations have this form: \(T(z) = e^{i\theta}(z-z_0)\text{.}\) Note that any Euclidean transformation \(T(z) = e^{i\alpha}z + b = e^{i\alpha}(z+e^{-i\alpha}b)\) may be written in this form.

If \(k=0\) the arc-length formula becomes

\begin{equation*} {\cal L}(\boldsymbol{r}) = \int_a^b \frac{2|\boldsymbol{r}^\prime(t)|}{1}~dt = 2\int_a^b |\boldsymbol{r}^\prime(t)|~dt \end{equation*}

which is twice the usual Euclidean arc length.

If \(k=0\) the area formula becomes

\begin{equation*} A = \int\int_R 4r~dr~d\theta \end{equation*}

which is 4 times the Euclidean area.

If \(k=0\) the triangle area theorem reduces to the (defining) Euclidean fact that the sum of the angles of a triangle equals \(\pi\) radians.
