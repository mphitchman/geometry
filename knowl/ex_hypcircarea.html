Example 5.4.2. The area of a circle in \((\mathbb{D},{\cal H})\).

Suppose our region is given by a circle whose hyperbolic radius is \(a\text{.}\) Since area is an invariant, we may as well assume the circle is centered at the origin. Let \(x\) be the point at which the circle intersects the positive real axis (so \(0 \lt x \lt 1\)), as pictured below. Then, by the distance formula

\begin{equation*} a = \ln\left(\frac{1+x}{1-x}\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Solving for \(x\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} x = \frac{e^a - 1}{e^a + 1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

This circular region may be described in polar coordinates by \(0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi\) and \(0 \leq r \leq x\text{.}\) The area of the region is then given by the following integral, which we compute with the \(u\)-substitution \(u = 1 - r^2\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} \amp \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^x \frac{4r}{(1-r^2)^2}~drd\theta\\ \amp = \left(\int_0^{2\pi}~d\theta\right)\left(\int_1^{1-x^2}\frac{-2}{u^2}~du\right)\\ \amp = 2\pi\left[\frac{2}{u} \bigg|_1^{1-x^2}\right]\\ \amp = 2\pi \left[ \frac{2}{1-x^2} - 2\right]\\ \amp = 4\pi \frac{x^2}{1-x^2}\text{.} \end{align*}

Replace \(x\) in terms of \(a\) to obtain

\begin{align*} 4\pi \frac{(e^a - 1)^2}{(e^a+1)^2} \cdot \frac{(e^a+1)^2}{(e^a+1)^2 - (e^a- 1)^2} \amp = 4\pi \frac{(e^a - 1)^2}{4e^a}\\ \amp = 4\pi \left(\frac{e^a - 1}{2e^{a/2}}\right)^2\\ \amp = 4\pi \left(\frac{e^{a/2} - e^{-a/2}}{2} \right)^2\text{.} \end{align*}
