Solution 5.4.8.1.

We prove by induction that the area of an \(n\)-gon in the hyperbolic plane consisting of hyperbolic segments, and whose \(n\) interior angles are \(\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \cdots \alpha_n\) is

\begin{equation*} A = (n - 2)\pi - \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i\text{.} \end{equation*}

Basis Step: \(n = 3\text{.}\) The area formula holds for triangles. This is TheoremÂ 5.4.9

Inductive Step: \(n \gt 3\text{.}\) Assuming that the area formula holds for all \(k\)-gons for \(k = 3, \cdots, n-1\text{,}\) we prove the formula holds for \(n\)-gons as well. Suppose an \(n\)-gon in the hyperbolic plane has vertices \(v_1, v_2, \cdots, v_n\) and that the interior angle at vertex \(v_i\) is \(\alpha_i\text{.}\) Call this \(n\)-gon \(P_n\text{.}\) Construct the hyperbolic segment connecting \(v_1\) and \(v_{n-1}\text{.}\) This partitions the \(n\)-gon into a triangle (\(\Delta\)) and an \((n-1)\)-gon (\(P_{n-1}\)) as pictured in the following diagram.

Note that in the figure the angle at vertex \(v_1\) has been expressed as \(\alpha_1 = \tau_1 + \gamma_1\text{.}\) Similarly, \(\alpha_{n-1} = \tau_{n-1}+\gamma_{n-1}\text{.}\) Now by the inductive hypothesis, the area of the triangle \(\Delta\) is

\begin{equation*} A(\Delta) = \pi - (\alpha_n + \tau_1 + \tau_{n-1}) \end{equation*}

and the area of the \((n-1)\)-gon \(P_{n-1}\) is

\begin{equation*} A(P_{n-1}) = (n-3)\pi - (\gamma_1 + \alpha_2 + \cdots + \alpha_{n-2}+\gamma_{n-1})\text{.} \end{equation*}

The area of \(P_n\) is thus

\begin{align*} A(P_n) \amp= A(\Delta) + A(P_{n-1}) \\ \amp= \pi - (\alpha_n + \tau_1 + \tau_{n-1}) + (n-3)\pi - (\gamma_1 + \alpha_2 + \cdots + \alpha_{n-2}+\gamma_{n-1}) \\ \amp= (n-2)\pi - ((\tau_1 + \gamma_1)+\alpha_2 + \cdots + \alpha_{n-2} + (\tau_{n-1}+\gamma_{n-1})+\alpha_n) \\ \amp= (n-2)\pi - \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i\text{.} \end{align*}
